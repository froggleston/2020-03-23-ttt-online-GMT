Why Participatory Live Coding?

Up and Down (10 min)
List some advantages and challenges of participatory live coding from both a learner’s and an instructor’s point of view.
  • Maria: you can see the speed that learners absorb and can slow down or speed up if necessary
  • give immediate results - thrill of making
  • disadvantages: working with different levels in the class.
  • Mark: Learners are activated to participate and will remember more. Learers will see mistakes by the instructor and learn from this. The instructor will have to type and talk at the same time and that may be challenging. 
  • Advantage: give an impression how coding looks like, prove that it's not some kind of magic and show that errors can happen and it's ok, and comment on the errors. Disadvantage: split attention, teacher has to concentrate on speaking and coding at the same time
  • From the learner's point of view: getting engaged and getting the "hands in the dirt" very quickly (pro). However, there can happen a moment when the learner is just copying without understanding (con). 
  • From the learner's view: it is easier to ask questions during the coding that would otherwise be omitted, it feels more relatable specially if teacher makes a mistake or googles something in the middle, or to try what would happen if we change something in the code and why it works or not 
  • From Learner's view: advantages: more interest on learning Challenges; shyness to ask  Instructor: more activeness, good output Challenges: time consuming
  • The learner would be more engaged and is practicing what they learn in real time
  • Possible technical difficulties, I have been to training sessions where many of the participants could not download the necessary software needed to be used due to the institution firewall/permissions 
  • Advantage for learner: "lowering threshold" by showing how it is done right away.
  • Disadvantage for instructor and learner: Instructor can be surprised by previous unencountered errors - cause for a lot of confusion for learners
  • Disadvantage for learner: Can sometimes be slower than just showing the code.
  • challenge for learners: making typos without realising and getting stuck on a certain line/piece of code (or other unexpected errors) 
  • advantage for learners: instructors may mistype, showing that it's no big deal and they are humans too 
  • Advantage: keeps people engaged, Challenge: hard to think, troubleshoot, and know where everyone is all the time, hard to guage your audience if you're busy
  • Advantage : learners do what you do so they learn how to do it by their own, it's pratical and useful,
  • Down : it's slow, you have to be in good shape as instructor to motivate the class
  •  
  • The instructor should be able to gauge, in real time, whether participants are able to keep up or not.
  • Students on the other hand can signal, using their sticky notes, whether they are able to keep up or not. Hopefully that mitigates the fear of sticking your hand up to say "I don't understand", or "I lost the plot"

Using Formative Assessments

Where are your checkpoints? (10 min)

Have a look at your lesson again. Choose a learning objective, and identify where in the lesson that objective should reasonably be achieved. How will you know that that objective has been met for all learners? Will this be clear to them?

Make a plan for where in your lesson you will use different types of formative assessment to help everyone in the room monitor their progress. Keep in mind that formative assessment can can take many forms, including multiple choice questions, faded examples, spontaneous questions and calls for sticky notes. 

Write some notes or thoughts about this process below for discussion.

Setting the Workshop Environment

Your Academic Past (5 min)
Think back to courses or workshops you really liked or didn’t like. 
  • How did those courses start on the first day? 
  • Were you confident in the instructors abilities? 
  • Did you feel like they were enthusiastic about the course and invested in you? 
  • Was it clear what you were going to be learning? 
  • Were you excited about the material? 
  • Did you leave that first day thinking the instructor was uninterested, that you weren’t the students they wanted to be teaching or you had no idea what the course was supposed to be about?

Sarah: workshops I liked: enthusiastic instructor, smiling, welcoming (the whole positive attitude, looking like she/he is so happy to be teaching you something). Good visual content: not too much text, some pictures and graphs (either on slide/page or live-drawn). Clear schedule described at the beginning, so you know what to expect and when. Workshops I don't like: instructor looking bored, speaking with a monotonous voice, telling that things are obvious when they are not at all to you. Instructors who look like they would like to be anywhere else but there teaching you. No or not enough hands on.
I like when a teacher does not stick to the hierarchy: teacher - student. I also appreciate if he/she is competent and confident. Besides, a good teacher feels the audience and adapts to it, monitor its response, even if it is non-verbal.
Jenny: I think I don't like courses that are too general. I get confused as to what I am supposed to be learning, or why it is useful to learn the material. I like clear, upfront objectives, especially for coding workshops, where it is easy to get lost in the weeds. I like when there is a story behind the data, and when instructors bring personal experiences to the table.
Mark: an enthousiastic instructor can make up for many other faults. So if the whole setup is a bit amateuristic, but the teachers is enthousiastic and able, it's usually still good!
Raphaela: one particular session comes to mind, where the instructor was very enthusiastic about the topic. He gave very nice motivating examples, went stept by step through different approaches. He was also present and involved in the labs, which I found especially engaging, since they were organised by his PhD student. By him being there and helping, I got the impression that he really cared about us understanding the material. I think many of us were really excited about the topic for some while after the course, I surely was.  
Rachel: I really like when a workshop introduction covers what I will get out of the class and what the teachers expectations are of the participants.  I also really appreciate understanding the real world application of the skills I'm going to be learning.  When I completed the Python software carpentries course the instructor was an expert and made alot of assumptions on people's prior knowledge of programming, the instructor dived straight in, it would have been nice to have some kind of icebreaker and understand some of the applications of what we were learning.  

Kristian: Courses/workshops with unclear prerequisites. I recently attended a workshop on how to use a AI node that our university had installed. The workshop was supposed to be on how to run the code on it but it apparently required rather in-depth knowledge of using shell, understanding virtual machine, understanding programming environments etc. This was not made clear and the instructor just wrote a bunch of shell commands without explaining what was happening.
Positive courses/workshops are mostly characterized by the fact that the instructor is passionate about the material. 
Maria: when instructor is enthusiastic and passionate about the material it makes the whole difference. The topic itself may be less of your interest but the energy is contageous.

Paloma: I really appreciate when the course begins with what to expect and what will not be covered, and brief description of the syllabus and resources (not very often). I appreciate when the introduction calls to the problem that we try to solve, so it puts a context to the topic because that makes it engaging. I disliked courses that inmediatly begin with heavy theoric background, very mathy unnecesarely and no relation to a real settings. 




What's in an Introduction? (10 min)
Get into small groups (3-4 people) and discuss the questions below for 5 minutes. Take notes on your answers.
  • What do you hope to accomplish in a workshop introduction?
  • What information do you need to include in an introduction to accomplish these goals?

Room 4: 
give people a good overview of what is going to happen in the course; set the stage --> You'll need the schedule
connect with people on a personal level; --> introduce yourself/helpers
get the right level of energy into the group, so that they are not too fast and not too slow; --> "leading" by example
in general, creating a good, productive atmosphere --> code of conduct as starting point

Room 1: Good first impression (Introduction of yourself: what experience you have, what you enjoy about coding, etc.) Ice breaker in groups / introductory activity. Ask them what are their expectations/goals for the workshop. Set up learning objectives and learning outcomes. Explain how it will be achived. Plan for the day.


Room 3: engage the students and make them feel enthusiastic about what's going to come
- what is going to be covered in the workshop
- name of the instructor and what your background is in the skill or topic being taught
- organisation of the day (when is the coffee? food?) when is the break
- expectations (including code of conduct)
- sticky notes and how they work
- emergency proceedures 
- reinterate that the level of the course is targeted for beginner 
- requirements (attendance/ participation requirements) 

Room 2: 
- Introduce instructors and helpers - including your own background and possible struggles with the content. 
- Introduce the environment, rules and use of sticky notes. 
- Cover the content and the goals and when there will be breaks. 
- Make clear when they can ask questions. 
- Making sure everyone is setup with the required software. 
- Introduce the tools: the etherpad, the page wth the materials.
- Demonstrate or explain the practical application and usefulness of what they are going to learn in the workshop

Practice Your Introduction (15-25 min)
Imagine you have completed instructor training and you are about to teach a full lesson around the material you have been practicing teaching today.
